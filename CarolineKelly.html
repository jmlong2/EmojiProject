Emoji Research

In her doctoral thesis "Do you know what I mean >:( : A linguistic study of the understanding of emoticons and emojis in text messages", Caroline Kelly delivered a questionnaire to a group of Swedish high school students. Within her handouts were questions asking the students to record their understanding of emojis used within example text messages. The question Kelly was asking with her research was whether or not a common understanding of emojis could be, or had already been, achieved.

What Kelly found was that the understanding of emojis used within text messages was highly dependent on context. Things such as who sent the message, how many emojis were used, what type of emojis were used, and the previous messages in the conversation all played crucial roles in helping the teens decode the emojis meaning in the fake messages. Kelly notes that "(t)he results differed very much when the participants tried to understand the context in which an emoticon/emoji was used. This indicates that the interpreter needs both a context and a reference, otherwise they must make assumptions as to what the sender of a message means with the emoji included after the text. There is, in other words, no such thing as a universal or individual understanding of the emoticons/emojis in themselves, as they need not only to be put in a context of whole text, but also in a context of what is being communicated" (25).

The way Kelly's study applies to the understanding of the overall project, I think, is that emoji use is dependent on a system (language) that we, by nature, cannot define. Lotman and Derrida, in their contributions to the fields of linguistics and semiotics, argue that language is context driven. Lanugage is driven by the idea that a definition can only be made in difference. As such, context is the only constant afforded to all types of communication. The idea of inserting facial expression and body language cues into text conversations as a retrieval of the obsolesced makes sense, because it offers a portion of the context which was previously lacking.
